Types of
transportation

In this section, you can learn about the various types of transportation available in Japan. Japan is renowned for its efficient and extensive transportation network, which includes trains, buses, subways, and more. Whether you're traveling within cities or exploring the countryside, Japan offers a wide range of transportation options to suit your needs.

  • Tickets and Passes
  • Shinkansen
  • Public transportation
  • Other

  • Regular tickets

    Regular tickets simply get you from A to B. The basic fare depends on the travel distance between your departure and destination station. The cost per kilometer is around 20 yen for short distances, and decreases to under 10 yen for longer trips.

  • IC cards

    There are ten major IC cards, including Suica and Icoca, that are interchangeably usable in most major cities in Japan. Furthermore, there are several minor IC cards whose usability is limited to local areas. IC cards can also be used for making purchases at many shops and restaurants across Japan.

  • Rail passes

    Rail passes entitle their holders to unlimited usage of trains in a designated area. There is a variety of nationwide and regional rail passes available in Japan. The most famous of them all is the nationwide Japan Rail Pass, but other rail passes can be more suitable, depending on your itinerary.

  • City passes

    Passes for unlimited city travel on subways, trams, trains and buses on one day and similar tickets are offered in many cities across Japan. Sometimes it is better than regular tickets.

  • Famous symbol of Japan

    The world-renowned high-speed passenger express combines speed, reliability, and unparalleled comfort. Shinkansen trains depart regularly from Tokyo and transport passengers throughout the country.

  • Types of Seats

    There are three types of seats: unreserved, reserved, and green cars.For detailed information on all available routes and fares, please visit the official website.

  • Types of Shinkansen

    There are three types of Tokaido-Sanyo Shinkansen: Nozomi ("hope"), Hikari ("light"), and Kodama ("echo"). Compared to the other two, Nozomi Shinkansen makes fewer stops. It's the fastest option for traveling to the Kansai region – to Kyoto, Osaka, Kobe, and beyond. Note: the JR Rail Pass is not valid on this specific route.

  • Local routes

    Local bus routes span the entire country, including remote areas. Major cities offer convenient unified fare systems for travel. Many buses accept prepaid transportation cards (IC cards) like Suica. Passengers typically board through the front door. Stop names are usually duplicated in English.In small towns, the fare for bus rides depends on the distance traveled. In this case, you should enter through the middle door. Upon boarding, take a ticket, and when you exit, check the fare for your ticket number — it will be displayed on the screen at the front of the bus. Place the money in the box near the driver and exit through the front door. Please note that payment with IC cards on such routes may not always be available.

  • Regional Passes

    In Japan, there are regional bus passes that allow unlimited travel within specific regions for a certain period of time. If you plan to travel extensively within one region, such a pass can be a wise purchase. The Inter City Bus Pass is the most economical way to travel around the Hokkaido region. The pass covers all major cities on the second largest island in Japan. The Sendai City Bus Ticket allows you to travel on buses within the city of Sendai for one day. The Tohoku Highway Bus Ticket offers unlimited bus rides in six prefectures in the Tohoku region. If you plan to travel extensively in the Kanto region, we recommend purchasing the Greater Tokyo Pass for unlimited travel on certain buses and trains. This pass is available only to foreigners.

  • Websites

    Many bus companies have websites in English where you can find up-to-date information and book tickets. Explore them to find the most suitable route.For example, the Intercity Bus Information Service.

  • Taxi

    Taxis in Japan are impeccably clean, and the level of service is high. Drivers do not deceive customers, and the fare is calculated by the meter. Like in most countries, taxis are more expensive than public transportation, but sometimes they can be more economical, especially if you are traveling in a group of three or four people. In most taxis in major cities, you can usually use credit cards, but outside the city, you will likely need to pay in cash. The signs under the windshield show whether the taxi is available. They are usually in Japanese but have color markings. Red means the taxi is ready to take passengers, while green indicates that it is occupied.

  • How much does a taxi cost?

    The fare is calculated based on the distance shown on the meter, except for some routes with a fixed rate to airports and major attractions. Taxi drivers in Japan will not try to deceive passengers and will use GPS and the meter to get you from point A to point B as quickly as possible. An extra charge for nighttime hours is taken from 10:00 PM to 5:00 AM, and all expenses for traveling on toll roads are paid by the passenger.

  • Bicycle

    In many hotels and private establishments in Japan, bicycle rental services are offered. Typically, rental offices are located near railway stations. A bicycle is an inexpensive and convenient means of transportation. To rent a bike, you will need a passport. In most rental points, maps and guides are provided. In more modern companies, there are electric bicycles, which are very convenient in hilly areas.
